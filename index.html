About

Hello, my name is Erica Ingram, and I'm a software developer. I live in Seattle, Washington, where I've owned and operated a court transcription business, A Quo Co., for a decade. Currently, I attend Lambda School.

I've always been a persistent person who never let a challenge get in the way of accomplishing a goal. When I was 14, my dad said, "Whatever you save for a car, I'll match it when you turn 16." My mom looked at him and said immediately, "You're going to regret that." Determined to get the coolest car I could, I got a job detasseling and asked my mom to take me every day, which she did.

Every day for two summers, she would drive me for 40 minutes to arrive at 6:00 in the morning and ready to go back home at 4:00, after having walked in corn fields with my arms above my head all day long. I saved up $1,900, not a small feat for a teenager, and Dad, true to his word and without regret, matched me. I was able to buy a used 1989 Ford Mustang black convertible, my name was the only one on the title, and I drove that until I sold it to come to Seattle.

Originally from a very small town in Iowa called New London, I moved to Seattle in July 2000 with a bus ticket, a guitar, a backpack full of clothes, $150 in my pocket, and a dream of living here. My friend and I had heard from her art teacher about a hostel here where we could get work, so off we went, and I did indeed work there for two years, much of that as their housekeeping manager and at the front desk. My parents and everyone thought I was nuts, that I would be back with my tail between my legs in two weeks, but here I am, still in the heart of Seattle 19 years later, still carless as well!

In 2005, my husband of now 14 years, whom I met while playing Wolfenstein: Enemy Territory, moved all the way from Newfoundland to be with me here, and we married in April 2006.

In 2009, I opened A Quo Co., and quit my day job in 2010. Being a solo entrepreneur, when it came to my company's website and workflow software, I took stock of my finances and decided it was worth it to learn how to produce these things on my own.

Something you might guess about court transcription is that there is a ton of administrative (unpaid) tasks and paperwork you must complete. As you also might guess, the admin stuff was extremely tedious and took up a lot of my time without any pay-off. In addition, people might stay in or be released from prison, maintain or lose custody of family members, or have other dire consequences as a result of what's in a transcript, so I cannot understate the importance of being thorough and accurate.

So that led me to learn VBA. At first, it was just a few macros and functions. Eventually, I learned Access and then wrote my company's database with MySQL. It does all kinds of things like interacts with various APIs over the internet, generates 25-30 different custom Office documents, auto-manages tasks for various court jurisdictions to ensure I've done everything required for that jurisdiction, manages my company's finances, auto-generates and sends e-mails and invoices as well as many other things. It's very robust now, although it certainly didn't start out that way.

A Quo Co. was the first and is the only company that hyperlinks legal authority in transcripts, and this idea came to me while learning VBA.

But learning VBA and developing my website made me realize how much I loved coding. So after receiving four certificates from freeCodeCamp, I decided to take the plunge with Lambda School, where I am currently enrolled in the full-stack web development track. I still own and operate my company alongside school and enjoy video games and hockey with my spouse.

Now you know my story, and if you want to check out my work, click here.
